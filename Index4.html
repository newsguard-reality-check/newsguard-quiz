How to Spot a Fake News Screenshot
Russian disinformation campaigns use fabricated article screenshots to spread false narratives. Here's what to look for:
  • 🔤
    Check the typography & formatting
    Real outlets have consistent font sizes, line spacing, and byline styles. Fakes often have mismatched fonts, awkward line breaks, or wrong logo treatments.
  • 🌐
    Search for the article directly
    Paste the headline into Google and add the outlet name. If the article doesn't appear on the outlet's actual website, it almost certainly doesn't exist.
  • 📅
    Verify the date and URL
    Look for a visible URL or date in the screenshot. Fabricated screenshots often omit these, or display a URL that doesn't match the outlet's actual domain.
  • 🖼️
    Reverse image search the screenshot
    Upload the image to Google Images or TinEye. If the screenshot appears across Telegram channels or fringe sites but not news databases, it's likely fake.
  • ⚡
    Be skeptical of claims that seem too dramatic
    Fabricated headlines are designed to provoke outrage or disbelief. If a claim seems shocking, that's reason to verify — not to share.
